There's a picture she took of me that first night in the apartment she'd rented under her own name and everything. I'm sitting on the typical apartment-colored carpet in the apartment-colored apartment's one and only bedroom. When we were kids, the teachers would tell us to sit "Indian-style", which was exactly how I was sitting. There is nothing else in the room except the sticks of a bedframe I'm assembling Jimmy McNulty-style like it's still Season One in The Wire but we've already somehow kept watching past Season Three and no longer care how it all ends anymore anyway. I'd been functionally homeless for a good month before I'm sitting on that chunk of crappy carpet and grinning like an idiot because I wasn't ever completely certain she would really come back. Let alone stay. She left me there for dead every single time she walked through and then out; the only decorations in the place were the ones she’d put there, including a vinyl decal that made the same false promise every single time I walked through and then out myself:

BREATHE: YOU’RE HOME.

The whole relationship was so full of anxiety it felt like I was constantly suffocating. Drowning. Which of course I was.

I was in “our” bedroom that terrible night she was still in her office late on a Friday night and I was driving all the way to see her in about 12 hours. The next day was a Saturday and it was the final day I had to drag my then-wife and three children to a church off Canyon Road so the state of Oregon was satisfied our life together was and needed to be over. I was the only one certain of it. That next day Saturday was the last Saturday ever any of us would have to spend; Don Henley’s Last Worthless Evening would fit if it wasn’t about someone else. I had an old iMac centered on the single dresser in the room with 4 drawers in it and only half of those had my shirts and socks and underwear in them because the rest of it was still packed of the things she’d brought and left, which still to me felt as if they were beamed in from outer space.

NO WISE MAN HAS THE POWER.

## JUST WAIT TILL TOMORROW / I GUESS THAT'S WHAT THEY ALL SAY / JUST BEFORE THEY FALL APART.
-NEW ORDER, 1993.
-----
She said she wanted our split to be “amicable,” to be handled with “grace.” She told me this in a text message exchange, our last one of any length or substance, which was the last conversation we had as a (barely) functioning married couple. “I am taking a few furniture items for my new place,” she texted me. “You can have the rest.”

Three weeks later she and her crew are gone from the house we’d lived in for not even a year before everything fell apart between us. I am alone when I go the house for the first time in over a month, with absolutely everything different between us. I walk through the gate she and I had built together last summer to keep the dogs in the house’s back patio area; the house sat on four full acres of evergreen trees and green sloping grass lawns, and coyotes and deer and a cougar now and then were far more frequent visitors than people, so letting even the big Newfoundland of ours wander around at will at dusk was largely out of the question. The big concrete area is a tidy mess of little piles of inconsequential things. I turn my housekey in the lock for maybe the first time ever since renting the place (I always entered through the garage), push back one of the big blue doors, and instantly feel a rush of negative energy so powerful it knocks me back for a second.

The house is almost entirely stripped of everything it once held. Gone are all of the beds, including the new one I’d bought not even a year prior, and another new one I’d bought for my son. I’d learn not too much later my son’s mattress had been taken by her as well, not relinquished to him when he moved out of the house with his younger brother, setting up their new homebase with their mother out in Hillsboro. As I moved from room to room, a haunt descended upon me that still even months later has not lifted, a haunt that has only intensified with the horrible silence that’s between us now, a silence I insisted upon when she terrorized me at work not even 48 hours after she told (texted) me that she had decided to file for divorce and wanted nothing ever to do or say to me ever again. This was in the same exchange, by the way, that she used the words “amicable” and “gracious”.

Downstairs was a realm she didn’t enter often. All of the boys were usually in residence down there, and she couldn’t manage the stairs too well because of an achilles heel condition she refused to have treated not long after moving out to live with me and my three boys. When I went down there that day, however, it was beyond clear she had taken a comb through every single thing down there, including my library, wherefrom she had plucked a signed first edition of INFINITE JEST that I had carted all the way to Normal, IL in 1996 when I started graduate school and began studying with David Foster Wallace. She had also taken a personal letter Wallace wrote to me years later, when he was still teaching at Pomona, as well as an essay I had written for him in a class I took with him in the spring of 1997. The essay wasn’t a good piece of writing, and Wallace’s notations all over it underscored the fact; it was a formative piece of trial and error in my education, an invaluable artifact from a time when I had so much to learn about writing, school, life, and family, and but so knowing that she had taken these things from me felt way beyond personal. My son, whose mattress she also absconded with, asked her about these items because before he’d moved out, he noticed they were missing. She lied to him and told him the items were being held in a security deposit box locally during the move so they wouldn’t get misplaced, and she promised to give him access to these things even though they didn’t belong to him, either. A few weeks after telling him this lie, she would ghost him permanently, when after her leaving the state no doubt with a big U-Haul full of all the things she promised to leave in the house, my son asked her where the family pets were, and in particular if he could have his own cat. She never answered him. Never has. Yet two weeks after his asking, she made sure to check in with his younger brother on that brother’s birthday, just to make her presence felt. That was the last time any of my children had any contact with her, a woman who had prided herself on playing the role of a kind of super-mom to them, but at the end of the day, she was neither their mother nor super.

## DON’T DISCARD ME JUST BECAUSE YOU THINK I MEAN YOU HARM.
-GEORGE + SIR ELTON, 1991.
-----

My nostrils instantly recognize the chemical perfume I'd instantly forgotten, tens of teens of years ago when we were still trying to be married and trying at the trying, which of course made it a constant gamble by default. The last time I smelled that sick, fat, saturated fake rose smell, my wife was literally on my arm and I could still literally claim to have something others called a wife to begin with. It was something like our 5th wedding anniversary but we'd never stayed a single night alone and this was that trying kind of trying so because my wife wasn't my first wife, she had to bribe her oldest daughter into babysitting the younger two, and but so the anniversary clock started running out way before we even got married in the first place.

The casino was maybe a full year old and didn't have its hotel and gas station yet. The casino was across the freeway from La Center, WA; our hotel was about 30 minutes north in Centralia, not quite yet to Olympia, and not even remotely out of concussion range from Mount St. Helens, which blew its top in the early 1980s and quite truly has never stopped. One of my classmates from Twin Falls, David George, the son and namesake of a practicing and apparently quite good dentist, got himself a PhD in some kind of geosciences and went to work right away on an active volcano and is still there as far as I know. I probably told her that on the way up and past and around and back. She would have already known what I was talking about anyway and known all that anyway because she was such an operator of that grand switchboard she kept clamshelled shut somewhere deep inside of her that I never even bothered to fathom, let alone try and reach.

The hotel was probably the shittiest in Centralia, which really does seem like a big boast to make if you've ever stopped for gas or had to eat at the Denny's on your way to/from somewhere way better like Seattle or Portland. Then again, I guess that would completely depend on what you mean when you use a word like "better", which isn't even remotely worth thinking about but no one seems willing to say this often enough for everyone to believe it the same way they do, say, gravity or sunsets. 

You know? Like bullshit?

I walk around the entire casino floor like four times. The sushi place whirls and whirls and whirls and whirls in the same spot. I try to remember what was here before, last time. But I don't at all try to remember what was here before THAT.

Not even a few weeks later, all the leaves are splattered that mustard yellow and falling to the ground. We never knew what to do once that November chill in the air stays permanent and doesn’t let go. Not even the next November, it doesn’t. And certainly not the next one, neither. Once that November chill drilled into my bones it stayed. Permanently resident. Residently present. 

It’s fifteen years later this November and everything aches, and not at all like it used to. The last warm day I can ever remember was the one where I watched my kids take baseball practice and complained about how hot it was and how far the car had to stretch that tank of gas that week but that was way before the summers cooled into how they are now:

Endless.
