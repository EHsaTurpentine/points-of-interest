The second I signed the lease on that farmlette in Damascus, I knew for certain it was The End. And when she lied about bringing her ex-husband there before we’d even got the keys to unlock those two huge blue front doors which swung wide enough open to easily pass through a piano, I knew I wanted her gone. And when I got home from just running down the hill to the Fred Meyer not even 1/4 mile away to find him already showering in our bathroom, I knew I wanted him gone, too.

We reaped what we sowed is another way to say it. For about 30 seconds there was a conversation about pasturing alpacas; instead, she squirted out more grand babies. For about 20 seconds, there was a conversation about her getting a job and going on interviews after she’d actually and finally gone on one but would never get one. ## THERE ARE TOO MANY WAYS THAT YOU CAN KILL SOMEONE LIKE IN A LOVE AFFAIR WHEN THE LOVE HAS GONE.
-NEW ORDER, 1987.
------

It’s summer and the sky is clear and we might have even been on the reservoir with the inflatable raft that could squish down in the back-back, where the hatch slams shut and makes the raft squeak and shake. The water below is on my mind. Like as in what happens when this Colt Vista 4X4 starts moving and I can’t stop it from the mental projection I’ve always had on these roads from the very beginning and infancy of my memory: cars and trucks and trailers in all sorts of carry plunging into the reservoir from hundreds of feet with the sheer side of the cliff falling to shale. When I rode on those roads as a kid, I thought they were the beginning of something and the river valleys, yeah, they’d been mined, but that was thousands of years ago and now the whole thing is brand new and undiscovered and most definitely unsettled. What I had no idea was those are the oldest roads in Idaho and that valley’s settlement predates Idaho’s very statehood. Idaho was nicknamed The Gem State after it officially became a state and was admitted a post-Civil War United States that has never been very post as far as domestic conflict and even more domestic violences. Idaho was nicknamed that because the only reason to be in Idaho at all as a white person in the late 1800s was to extract something or squat on a thin square of sage, just to continue pulverizing the few Bannocks left now at Fort Hall and forever thumb noses at the Shoshone. 

I don’t know what the Bannock, Shoshone, Paiute, Umatilla, etc etc called these rivers or these places. I don’t know it now and I never knew it growing up and spending huge chunks of my childhood, all 4 seasons, exploring that valley not on horses and not on foot but of course with off-road vehicles. When I was 7, I was given my first motorcycle; it, too, was a custom-built machine Frankensteined together with parts from other things that may or may not have been motorcycles before they got assembled and presented as what sounded and handled like a chainsaw with handlebars. MFUP presented it to me on the front porch of that house on Jefferson St. He was incredibly proud of what he’d made. MMAB had been assured what he’d built was perfectly safe and The Kid was teachable even if a candy ass and but so it would be just fine. Which it wasn’t. Instantly. When the candy ass kid didn’t learn instantly enough and plowed MFUP’s pretty little hate machine into the chain link fence underneath all those apricot trees, it most definitely wasn’t. That all happened back in Twin Falls, though. In an actual neighborhood with asphalt streets instead of clay and gypsum ones that had to be oiled in the summer time and scraped by huge snow plows in the winter time. When I was in grade school I remember a very brief conversation about moving up to Featherville permanently and I would have to be bused every day to not-nearby Mountain Home, home of the Strategic Air Command’s combined tactical forces of F-111s, F-16s, B-52s, F-18s, and that was back then; only god really knows what all they have by way of munitions there. I would have certainly said I didn’t want to, if asked. What’s funny now is, I don’t recall them ever asking. Either time. Or, really, I guess, pretty much any time. 

My earliest fears stemmed from watching the cars ahead of us on these old, narrow timber and mining roads, winding their way around the reservoir. Every picosecond a car didn’t miss its turn or slide down a cliff was a never-ending loop of anxiety and relief; once we arrived at the cabin itself, I was always relieved. Every single time. In the summers, I’d “get” to ride in the back of the truck sometimes completely by myself. The first time I can remember doing it, I had a Spider-Man comic book I’d got at the grocery store, off the spinning rack there at either the 7-11 just across Filer or the Safeway on the west end of Main; the cover promised Spidey would battle the Will O The Wisp, but that memory locked in immediately as another character and I’ve never understood why. 

The Wendigo.

I’ve never understood why. And still don’t. And almost certainly never will.

I didn’t ever believe in god when I was a kid. But I nonetheless still said little prayers and made little deals with myself every second there wasn’t a rock slide or avalanche or disaster of some kind. Prayers and deals that it really was going to be okay and we really would make it to our destination, what whole Destiny thing Manifesting, even, if you will.

“Just don’t put it on the bottom of the reservoir,” he chuckled. MMAB slugged him somewhere on the arm or chest or back or head or wherever she could slug him when he said it. She was right behind me, in the 2nd row seating that had its own doors for entry and everything. Sandy and Brandy, the two dogs, were either right beside her or more likely crawling all over and on top of her like bacteria like usual. I don’t remember where the dogs were but they would have been in there too because MMAB didn’t ever want to leave them anywhere or anytime she didn’t have to. 

“There’s a town down there, you know,” he said as he rubbed his arm or whatever. He’d stopped chuckling but the tone was still light. I guess. 

“I don’t feel like driving around that old place TODAY though, eh Bonn?” He wasn’t talking to me. He usually wasn’t. He might have chuckled a single chuckle more but that would have been it because there was no more slugging.

It wasn’t the first time I worked a clutch, or the first time I drove a car. What it was, though, was the first time doing either of those things had weight and consequence; I knew I could drown us that day, that I had that kind of power in my feet and fingers, to get to the bottom and see Old Pine Grove, ID for myself. But not that day, apparently. 

Not that day.

## I DON'T KNOW WHERE I CAN GO TO LET THESE GHOSTS OUT OF MY SKULL / MY GRANDMA'S PASSED MY BROTHER'S GONE / I NEVER AT ONCE FELT SO ALONE.
-ARRESTED DEVELOPMENT, 1992.
-----

It's impossible to overstate how bright the sunlight is on the river banks, amidst all that rubble. 

In August, the Kokanee swim all way back here from the very edge of the ocean, as they are freshwater salmon; all of the Boise River--as well as its sister and namesake, the Salmon River, not terribly far to the east and the gold rush in Idaho's TRUE POINT OF BEGINNING--is spawning grounds for Kokanee; legends have it, at one point the rivers were more full with spawning Kokanee than water, so those wishing to cross from north to south or east to west or vice versa or vice versa could step or slide across, atop the slimy heads and backs of those flame-red fish; the river boiling bloody with them all.

I think we went to the riverbank once and looked down together, she and I did on that solitary trip we took there to make up and reconnect. I think we did because I have a photograph of the bank but not of her there upon it. 

The prepositions, they always matter way more than the pronouns. 

I don't remember much by way of details. But we probably held hands the whole way down there because there wasn't anyone else around; she probably smiled that craggy smile of hers and then said something to totally ruin the entire romance of the moment. 

We'd started off writing daily emails--"love letters," I guess--to each other, but she’d already got way bored + behind + finally just quit. And by the time we'd got married, all that had changed and was displaced by the constant macro- and micro-dramas that ate up every ounce of affection and patience we could muster for each other and ourselves, too. 

And by the time it all imploded, I couldn't stand being in the same time zone as her anymore, let alone the same house with the same sames.
