## WHEN ALL THAT’S LEFT TO DO IS REFLECT ON WHAT’S BEEN DONE THIS IS WHERE SADNESS BREATHES THE SADNESS OF EVERYONE.
-LIVE, 1994.

It was about the time you dragged your ass back there they renamed that stretch of Highway 20 a Medal of Honor highway, like all the Highway 20s everywhere else. Cutting right through Bannock country like that. Damming up. Flooding an entire town. Like Celilo later or about the same time or what's the fucking difference, living a life of such prosperity among the conquered + vanquished?

There's a stretch of highway just past Fairfield we drove enough that I don't remember a time in my childhood that I wasn't traveling Highway 20. And that same stretch of highway has a spot where the railroad tracks cut through; it was super late on a Sunday night during a time of the year when you shouldn't be outside doing anything on a Sunday night, when the snow can drift feet high within a matter of minutes once the wind really picks up and howls across that entire valley. At Fairfield, a runt of a ski hill sprouted up after the war--Solider Mountain--built up on the north hills of that Bannock valley where the last bits of resistance faded into a terrible silence.

That Night Of The Black Ice, I knew for sure I would be dead someday. And on That Night, I very much thought it would be right then. Not on a Someday.

Decades later, we'd retrace the whole path there and back, exit to exit, asphalt to gravel to dirt to sand to water. There was a Bruno Mars song somewhere in the soundtrack of it. We stopped at the town's only gas station to refuel your Denali and ate our gas station sandwiches and drank our gas station sodas just down the block from the gas station, parallel parked next to the single orange traincar designated to be the county's historical center. I don't remember a gazebo just like the one my parents would build the following spring-into-summer on their own multiacre hobby ranch just downstream the Snake, near Jerome; I don't remember how the roads most places were always still just dirt roads and had deep ruts in them to match the ruts in their makers; and I don't remember what time it finally was when we arrived, having circled and circled the reservoir until the dirt clouds calmed from all the switchbacks; I don't remember how many times you looked back to watch them fall, if you did; and I most certainly don't remember a single detail about your body that night as the roof popped and popped and popped. 

What I do remember is you taking a picture of me with your phone as I returned to the bed that next morning. Later you got on the same phone and used some app to draw a word balloon with the characters D and 2 and F in them, as if either of us spoke in heiroglyphics or something.

But before that End there was that time on Alturas Drive when I got out of the car and refused to get back in.

And but before that End there was a time in Alturas County when Hailey wasn’t its capital.

And but before that End is where time in the 208 roughly begins. The state had a single area code for most of the 20th century, until the proliferation of cell phones broke through all that xenophobia and rendered all of the area codes effectively meaningless. Landlines were the only lines of instant communication for a fairly short blip of time in human history; Alexander Graham Bell is stringing telephone wire across rooftops well after gold is discovered near Riggins on the Salmon River; Florence, ID is but one of dozens if not not hundreds of mining towns that sprung up like mold along the riverbanks of Bannock, Paiute, and Shoshone hunting/gathering grounds since time immemorial.

That 208 area code, derived from the original 208 million acres of what was Idaho Territory before the 1860s, Idaho Territory which contained all modern day states of Idaho, Wyoming, and Montana combined, with Fort Lewiston on the Clearwater Water; in 1889, the Territorial government would create Idaho’s first and only land grant university at Moscow, just 8 crow miles from the Washington border, where a bigger sister town named for its owner/operator Mr. Pullman, which had its own land grant university imposed upon it from its own Territorial government way west of all that. 208 MILLION acres; and all of it east of the Cascades.

It’s in the 1860s when Hudson’s Bay comes calling for the gold, too, having already set up shop with the New Perce in the north a number of centuries before all the wars would wipe it all clean. Hudson’s Bay in 2025 is nothing more now than an intellectual property; it has gone out of business, and its word marks and logos are now owned by a Canadian telecom of some sort or something like that. 

I’m not making this part up, either. The part where a private company sets up shop for 500 years, causing all kinds of agony and mayhem as no fewer than four competing governments are yanking as much of the consumables off the continent as fast as they could be extracted; probably my single favorite video game of the 1990s was one I discovered when I befriended a computer science major at the University of Idaho who lived on my dorm. The game was Sid Meier’s CIVILIZATION, which then evolved/iterated/versioned to COLONIZATION, wherein I got to play resource manager and decide which tribes would be subjugated and which would survive.

In 1864, the gold rush is well underway in the Sawtooth Mountains, as is the subjugation of the Bannocks, Shoshones, Lemhi, Nez Perce, and Paiutes, all of whom had known the mountains, rivers, lakes, drainage basins, and valleys for literally all of human history as the sort of home us owl-faced fuckos will largely never understand, let alone respect: HOME as a natural spirit that echoes across spaces, and doesn’t get trapped in any one particular for any particular amount of time.

As she and I sat on the tailgate and ate our convenience store sandwiches, I still had no idea any kind of war had or would ever be fought and bled and rotted out right where we were sitting. Fairfield’s roads aren’t paved; they are the same gypsum-dirt that’s everywhere in the valley, ground up from all the excavation and mining that started in full production in the 1850s.

Fast forward to the 1950s, and the Indians are all gone. The camas prairie never became a reservation for them, that special kind of prison so very well known to all of us who had the misfortune (MALHEUR!!) of being born on the Snake River Plain; the difficult fact that we were born WHITE + SO VERY FUCKING SOON after Buffalo Horn raided and sank Glenns Ferry, that it was so fucking soon enough to be so well-known as to be an unspoken kind of well-known. To grow up white and privileged in the 1970s like I was took a certain kind of “Magic,” indeed, and for all I knew as a proud son of Twin Falls, ID was that time itself began with the founding of the town in the early 1900s. In 1984, MFUP and MMAB purchased one of the town’s first actual, permanent residences. MMAB spent little time and whatever expense it took to have the house we lived in properly recognized by the Idaho Historical Society as “Guy Seier’s House” or whatever it got called, after the dude who’d moved to TF from somewhere else like every other owl-faced fucko who’d taken up roost to scavenge what was left of the Shoshone and Paiute empires.

## DON'T WANNA DO YOUR SLEEPWALK DANCE ANYMORE.
-EVERCLEAR, 1995.
-------------------

That day I fell into the river and knocked my head on its bed, I was snagged at the wrist when the cheap gold bracelet I was wearing caught a river rock as the water sped over with me in an inner tube floating until the very second we’re not which means I’m not which means there’s water in every direction filling every thing and it’s all I see and all I hear and it’s ALL AROUND and it’s COLD and it’s COLD and it’s COLD because it’s the first real hot days of summer up here at altitude which means this water was still snow mere days/hours/minutes ago. 

That day I thought I could and should and would die. Not just probably. Certainly. If not that day, another day. Even more certainly. For certain, even.

And the river, that day, it spat me back.

The Little People, maybe.

The Fire-Carrier, maybe.

The inner tube isn’t the issue. The speed of the water, neither. The depths, neither; at most places in the summers, because of the dredging, there are stepping stones literally EVERYwhere. If you want to stand up and walk across, using the dregs from the dredge or across on the backs of the spawning Kokanee, dazed and dying and probably already dead like the spirits beneath, be my guest. And theirs, too. 

Any always that doesn’t care if you are aware of it is an always that is truly forever.

## IN THIS LIFE YOU'RE ON YOUR OWN.
-PRINCE & THE REVOLUTION, 1984.
-----------------------

Driving west on Addison with visible flames trailing from the engine compartment, which, of course on VW Beetles is in the back. Addison Ave intersects with Shoshone Ave and Blue Lakes Blvd at West Five Points, which is like 5 blocks max from the house on 8th Ave N where my parents had the lights off for the night and the doors and windows all locked because Somebody Has To Work You Know. Right there, West Five Points. It’s a Sunday night, I’m almost positive of it. There’s some weird flashing in the dash before my tape deck and matching amp completely die and then the headlights flicker and the car is now rolling towards and into the intersection. I looked into the rearview mirror and I don’t know what prompted me to look but the flames I saw prompted me to get the fuck out of there which I very promptly DID. I’m sitting on the south side of Addison, over by where the Popeye’s would go later and KLD with it, and Darrel is sitting with me, just sitting there and helping me calm the fuck down. Darrel and I know of each other and were friendly in junior high when we had gym class together and I had my first real crush on a girl I had never met before and that girl was Darrel’s half-sister Angela and every single boy I knew in 7th grade had a crush on Darrel’s half-sister Angela, and I’m quite sure a whole bunch of 8th and 9th grade boys did, too. It’s funny to think of how kind and gracious she was in deflecting my interest, and even moreso how kindly and graciously I accepted her disinterest in aforementioned interest. She could literally have her pick of the boys at O’Leary within 2 weeks tops of school starting. She was so used to having to turn boys away, she was obviously already pro at it by 6th grade. Angela loved Rick Springfield her entire life and when she was in a coma after a terrible motorcycle accident, Rick heard about it and recorded a video for her to watch when she woke up but she never did. Darrel, though, he was right there for me exactly and precisely where and when I needed him. I will always be grateful for that, too. Thank you, Darrel. And, again, I’m so so so very sorry for your loss. She was, in a single word, angelic.

When I called the house I knew whoever answered would be pissed because it was late and dark and colder weather and MFUP down for night/count already. It took him longer than I thought it would to get there after I got him on the phone. He was unpleasantly surprised for sure to see the whole scene with intersection blocked and the VW he’d built drowning in all that cold hydrant water. All that volume. All that pressure. Force. 

But before that, it’s us on a hillside of sand and gypsum, high above the Anderson Ranch Dam, high enough to see water for a fair amount of where I’m looking, and that’s saying something in this part of the world for sure. High above the dam, MFUP is telling me about the OLD town of Pine Grove. I think it’s just the 3 of us and the dogs too and I don’t know why we are day tripping around the reservoir but MFUP pulls the car over to a wider part of the shoulder and turns the ignition so the Vista turns completely off. He looks at MMAB and she just shrugs her shoulders and then next thing I know, I’m behind the wheel of MFUP’s daily driver, and mostly what I’m thinking: if I break this car somehow out here in the middle of fucking nowhere, it will be hell on at least 18 levels; there aren’t enough levels in Dante’s Inferno, is what I’m saying.

He gets in on the passenger side and is already kind of kidding but mostly not, like he pretty much always did. It was hard to take him seriously all the time and he liked keeping people guessing. My parents would always fight about lots of things, and one those things was his use of sarcasm and more fatalist language, which has always seemed just what married people do, like a mutual, analog doomscrolling or something.

But anyway, yeah. We’re there on that inclined road, nose of that Colt Vista 4X4 and he’s daring me to put it in gear.

Goading me even.
