## GARAGE GLAMOROUS NOT SURE WHAT IT MEANS BUT THIS PHOTO OF US IT DON’T HAVE A PRICE.
-GAGA, 2008.

That shit with the ziptie back in that Oregon City treehouse was never fucking funny. Not for a fucking second. Something terrible had already happened to the oldest living with us at the time; for something even worse to the youngest, would have been impassable.

We could all feel it in the room when he was turning purple and no one knew how to turn him back pink. The scissors wouldn’t fit and a straight blade was way too dangerous given the circumstances but mostly given the swelling in the kid’s neck. What’s that place PAST fear, by the way? Where you aren’t IN danger anymore? That there isn’t a threat of something terrible happening? That the terrible thing is right there with you, IN you, even, moving THROUGH you (you hope?), like the thorniest, nastiest anything that’s ever had to pass through you.

A lot of the innocence we’d somehow saved up to then was shattered. The child turning purple was my son regardless of whose blood was pooling in his neck and whose names were on his birth certificate. He was my son. And he was dying. And I knew it. And his mother knew it, too. She knew it so well, so couldn’t speak anything coherent she was so terrified by the moment. 

I did my best to be calm as I maneuvered the tin snips against his neck. I knew we’d get one chance at this and there wasn’t time to call paramedics, even though a major hospital stood atop the hill our house was on, not even 6 blocks away. I knew the pressure could asphyxiate him. What I knew most of all was that we were already out of time.

When the plastic snapped and his face bleached back out, her first reaction past all the hugging and crying and DON’T YOU EVER AGAINs was to call the boy’s REAL father and cry the largest cries I ever saw her cry and have never forgotten and almost certainly won’t. 

Because she was apologizing.

Over 

And

Over.

The person on the other end of the phone was an entire time zone and hundreds of miles away and also the REAL father of the boy who almost strangled himself to death when he made a necklace out of a pile of zip ties he’d found in the garage. The person on the other end of the phone was a story and phantom, mostly. But nevertheless REAL. And my new wife, his then-ex one, was wailing and apologizing for “almost killing our son.” 

Fact remains that we had saved THEIR son together that day, she and I did. She was holding the boy and trying to get him to hold still and calm down just long enough for me to get the very tip of those snips to pass between the thin ribbon of plastic burying itself into all that swelling skin. The boy was looking completely the other direction, just like we’d told him to; I’ll never forget the look in her eyes before she turned them away and the same direction as the boy in her arms. That look said it all. And I would describe it to you in all the detail I can remember, how glassed and red and swollen they were already, how it was so very clear she was completely terrified in the moment and she never showed this side of her on purpose. I would describe it to you if I could. I can’t. 

More than that, though: I don’t want to.

When she was off the phone and calmer I asked why she apologized.

No, wait. That’s not right. I didn’t ask.

THAT’s right.

## AND I DIDN'T WANNA WRITE A SONG.
-BIEBER, 2015.
------

McK was her oldest son and the first to make her a grandmother. And that raccoon, he never stood a fucking chance. Or its family, the entire mess of raccoon thieving bitches, they never stood a fucking chance.

Thieving aint swapping.

Nothing’s for keeps.

Ask that raccoon whose carcass we scraped out of the tree stump, chunk by bloody-hairy chunk, with the blunt end of that dead-headed arrow he’d found in the garage, conveniently missing its quiver and bow.

We took my old Chevy Blazer his half-sister would abandon there, right next to the stump she did. But that would be later. McK dug the rest of the carcass out from the tree stump and threw it into the big black yard bag I’d brought in triplicate from the house and not the garage. The raccoon had made the fatal mistake of hissing at my mini English bulldog, Daisey, and in a lot of parts of the world, that is more than plenty to get yourself killed. The bag was heavy and the carcass was a lot bigger than I’d first thought and even bigger than Daisey. 

We drove down the hill and onto Highway 99, passing under the Glenn Jackson Bridge and looking for a dumpster. It was a Sunday night and I want to say it was late winter or early spring of 2013. McK was spinning and living temporarily with us as Kaiser Permanente tried to use its resources and my coverage to treat his lifelong mental illnesses. McK as far as I know has never met his biological father, who is serving life sentences at the Snake River Correctional Facility in Ontario, OR for assaults and rapes. After I asked his mother to marry me, we all went to dinner at Gertie’s in TF and McK asked me if I was his father. I remember saying something along the lines of “I wish!”, making sure to lilt my voice up an octave when I said it, even. McK was either just 18 when I met him or turning 18 that first full year she and I were “together” but living hundreds of miles apart and pretending like we knew what we were doing. 

I still remember hearing that huge SPLAT when McK tossed that big black bag into a dumpster somewhere on River Road in Gladstone or Milwaukee. We’d decided together what to do with the bag and even where we wanted to dispose of it. McK’s eyes were big and bright and a ringer for his momma’s if his momma was Zac Efron instead of Trailer Park Barbie. He plopped down in the Blazer’s passenger seat and slammed its creaky door shut. “GO! GO! GO!” he yelled through a huge, toothy smile. Bigger, toothier, healthier, whiter, angrier than any toothy smile he might have inherited.

I clenched the fingers in my right hand into a fist and slid my thumb over them before extending it to McK. I must have had a toothy smile on my face, too, when I did it. 

McK did the same with his own right hand and we bumped our fists together and I felt his thumb hit my thumb when it happened.

“Now let’s get THE FUCK out of here and go back HOME,” he crowed.

“You got it, boss,” I said. Because that’s what he preferred calling me most of the time. Because he never knew what to call me in the first place + vice versa.
