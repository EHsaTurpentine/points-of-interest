## ‘CAUSE BABY YOU COULD DRAG ME TO HELL AND BACK JUST AS LONG AS WE’RE TOGETHER / AND YOU DO.
-WHAM!, 1984.

We'd rented that place in Oregon City already but she hadn't moved all the way over yet; she was still coming to visit by herself, and we still spent more time slapping each other's skin than we would ever realize. Especially at the time. That first full night she spent in the house, we slid down the big hill to the Home Depot just past where the Oregon Trail really and truly ended. I remember us holding hands walking in; we were, in fact, engaged and soon to be married and everything. When we got to the floor coverings section, I pushed her into a hanging row of those big floor rugs you'd put in a living room or at the foot of an enormous timberplanked staircase exactly like the one in the house we would all be living in together, children and pets and baggage and trauma all wound up and around and through like one of those balls made of rubber bands that you've deliberately cut half-way through just to see what it might look like. I pushed her like a lover pushes their lover from a public space into a private space so the public space can completely go away; I pushed her like that because she was still my lover and I thought she still enjoyed the private. So when she pushed me back and looked all embarrassed to be in public with me, it was the first and last time I ever thought I could suffer to feel that way ever ever ever again.

Mourning a place isn’t easy to do. Or admit. Places aren’t just places, is most of the problem. Places are also made of people. Not to mention our attachments and resentments to and for them. Places like Idaho. But PEOPLE. Right, right. We’re talking really about PEOPLE now, aren’t we, when we’re talking about grief. And I’m not so sure we actually ever mourn people, as much as the places we can never return without them. 

She did manage to teach me a thing or two. Nothing good. One of those things was that there’s a particular and peculiar kind of love that embraces the rose, thorns and all. Thorns first, even. That kind of love, it can feel bottomless. Because it is. Nothingness feels bottomless, too. For exactly the same reason.

I didn’t mean to lose that Aerobee that day at the reservoir; the metal ring in it took it instantly to the bottom, I’m quite sure. The beach was shards of granite and quartz, and like BIG shards, not soft, pulverized sand that’s all the same shade of browns-to-whites, like at the Sea of Cortez where we snorkeled and stayed wary of sharks spotted nearby. But that was Mexico, not Southern Idaho, and Bannock Territory at that. The Shoshone would be further eat, and your husband would drive drunk their buffalo grounds and camas fields at least twice a day. EVERY day.

Nope, that day on the reservoir, we were what looked like a functional family. The eight of us. Way more than enough.

I’d tossed the ring out as far as I could from where I stood on that rocky beach, thinking it just float on the top of the water after it plopped there, just bobbing on the surface and waiting eternally patient for one of us to get there in time to retrieve it before a thunderstorm or lightning strike. But the truth is: none of us even bothered to enter the water when we saw it disappear. And another truth is: we were all happy to just let it sink.

## I AM AN ARMS DEALER FITTING YOU WITH WEAPONS IN THE FORM OF WORDS / AND DON'T REALLY CARE WHICH SIDE WINS.
-FALL OUT BOY, 2007.
---------------

Never for a single second did I think I ever got her for keeps.

She belongs to the wind, if anything. If she's anyone.

We'd got groceries and then parked over by the railroad car. We'd woke up that morning at her house with her kids right next door. She'd decided we could get back together; why, exactly, I still couldn't even begin to guess.

She had that spectacularly terrible ruby ring on that she'd bluffed with at the Fred Meyer when she was visiting one time, just to check my reactions. And never for a single second did she think she'd have to stay past that part, the part she spent that entire weekend sneaking texts and stalling along the highway; we ate from the back of her SUV because she already knew the cell reception would suck up on Little Camas Prairie once we actually got there, and once you're past the prairie and into the landslide stopped by that reservoir there, there's none until Pine and then none again until Featherville and absolutely zero in Rocky Bar or Atlanta, all of which you can't in any honesty or with any seriousness call "towns." 

It wouldn't be even 72 hours later and I'd be standing above the dredge pond at Featherville there like an idiot after walking the half mile or so to get there, way past that BAKERS ACRES sign, hoping to catch the cell signals that were best caught early mornings and later evenings. Every couple of hours I'd go there and wait, right by the streetlight next to what used to be Featherville's single working gasoline pump, owned and operated by The Store there (self-service gasoline in Idaho, by the way, is considered an inalienable right every bit as important as the 2nd Amendment and probably way more so, honestly). If I didn't walk and drove instead because it was so early or so late in the day, I'd sit in the car above the dredge pond where the Old Dragon rotted up and had only a few scales left on its carcass in that shallow pond it died in back in the 1920s. What the last shift on that rig must have felt like, I don't have to guess. That feeling is all over that valley, drowned below Pine at Pine Grove, ID, population: reservoir/goodbye/ghosts.

My grandparents had their little camping trailer for much of my childhood parked on a parcel of land they'd bought alongside my parents'. They didn't go up to Featherville nearly as often as my parents did, or as much as parents thought they should; truth probably was: the older my grandparents got, the more they drank. And the more they drank, the worse they fought. And the worse they fought, the more they drank. You get the idea, I'm sure. I think I was still in grade school when they stopped coming entirely and sold that little chunk to my parents, whose real estate deals always had curious timing and thin-to-none forewarning; not that those deals were any of my business or anything, but the first house my parents bought was the last house MMAB lived in with her own biological parents.

I have a handful of memories of my maternal grandparents, and maybe a grand total of 5 of them being in that part of the world. One of them is sitting around the firepit MFUP had fashioned from an old irrigation cylinder; I want to say the first one was concrete but that's probably wrong; the one I remember most fondly was ringed by river rock spat out decades ago by The Dragon as it inched its terrible way up the Boise River's South Fork a good century ago. My grandparents sat around the campfire with us, drinking and chain smoking unfiltered Pall Malls by the packs until sometimes way past midnight, and in summertimes it got light and warming from freezing (it freezes nearly every night in Featherville year-round, regardless; it was nothing to see triple digit temps in the summers, too, and then the temps would plummet a good 20 degrees instantly as soon as the sun passed behind the mountains--which are in EVERY direction. Thunderstorms were the most intense weather I can remember up there, when the skies ripped open with barely seconds of notice; lightning storms and wildfires, if you would ever bother to ask a Bannock, Shoshone, or Paiute, are quite frequent and natural and completely unavoidable in this part of their world, the sky purpling up like a bruise that's shifting more and more into black than purple. That crisp shimmer the quaking aspen leaves made, silver half-dollars flashing all over at twilight or when the sun punched through all that bruising and calmed the very heart of everything the sharp beams touched. Instant warmth when atop you, bright and big and nothing else in the world BUT that light.

Like how it felt to be her entire focusing point of interest. I can't explain this. I used to wish I could, but too much happened and too much silence continues because of all that happened. The details matter. But not enough. The truth is almost certainly deeper and longer than any amount of knowing that has ever been known.

The entire intermountain NW is a tinder box in the summer time. And thunderstorms are a southern Idaho specialty. My parents almost lost their cabin to forest forest, three times in the last full decade they owned and operated it. The Bannock and Shoshone, who ruled this land for centuries, knew it to be particularly prone to enormous purging fires, well before owl-faced fuckos like Yours Truly came with Territorial Idaho's gold rush in the 1850s. Unlike us owl-faced fuckos, the Bannock and Shoshone didn't have ugly, faceless policies for thinning the forests (or, for that matter, cutting jagged roads all over the mountainsides, and/or dredging up any riverbed that The Dragon decided to plop its huge, square ass into); instead, for centuries, lightning struck freely and was allowed to do so; rather than helicoptering bucket after bucket of fresh snowmelt onto the flames, the Bannock and Shoshone left nature to run its course, leaving the land and lightning to themselves to work out their own differences. A bolt of lightning, see, it cannot exist if it doesn't strike the ground; to deny the bolt's existence isn't to rid the world of fire, but to supercharge--if not outrightly guarantee--its purifying return.

## KNOW THAT YOU'VE BEEN GOOD TO ME NOW I NEED YOU NOT TO BE.
-U2, 2001.
-------

Charlie Chaplin, the unequivocal first real master of a performance and recording medium that he largely invented—YES THAT CHAPLIN, whose visage still rules the entire film industry well over a century later—had this to say about what we do with the truth when it’s a double-headed dirge and it’s already tickling whatever hair you have left on the top of your head: 

## AS WITH ALL TROUBLE, ONE CANNOT BE CONSISTENTLY SERIOUS ABOUT IT.

When the bad guys are tying up the poor and helpless souls to the train tracks, there’s this Snidely Whiplash thing in silent films when the bad guy has to pause and pull on his handlebar mustache and says something to the camera or to another character within frame. The bad guys say things but you can’t hear them. You have to keep watching—and also READING—if you want to know how the story really goes and, of course, how it will really end.

Here's what I think I know now: I might also be what I knew then. It's all been dry erased away, though, so none of this matters. But you know that already.

I think I know now: we were never in love. We had to pretend to be, though, which is way way worse. The way we loathed each other at the very end was probably my favorite part, the way you'd already plenty of times already shown me YOUR TEETH.

I think I know now: we told ourselves we had better things to do than what we were doing but we did them anyway. There was never any stopping us. You know why, too. We're both ONLY CHILDREN.

I think I know now: we completely and unapologetically used each other. Brutally. Willfully. The worst kinds of ways: with intentions + without regards to the consequences. 

I think I knew then, though, I'd only ever just end up wasting my breath; you at least pretended to listen + lie right back to me. And then you'd laugh that laugh I couldn't ever stand but because you smiled that pixie smile and your skin smelled nothing like your smile, I couldn't ever hear you giggle anyway. You were always laughing at me, not with me. 

Or.....what? Did you think I never noticed?
