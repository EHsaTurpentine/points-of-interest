## IT DOESN’T MATTER TO YOU IT MATTERS TO ME.
-U2, 1991.

You’d staged it perfectly, packed every molecule of the 5 acres with the fury you felt. It was the first time I’d been back to the property since that terrible morning all the wheels came off. I was driving a completely different car and I was already a completely different person. I knew when I pushed the gate we’d hung there together, I wasn’t ready.

Because I was never ready.

My lawyer had told me it might be a good idea to try to go back earlier than this, before she’d left town, because that way you’d save fees down the road. Right. That’s why at the end of the day, I’d years later settle my malpractice lawsuit with that same lawyer and hasten his exit from a 40 year career in family law as the nepotistic egomaniac he so clearly was. I’d always refused to go back to the property, that entire six weeks living out of a single suitcase and wearing the same pair of shoes and same pair of jeans for the better part of two months and making absolutely certain I would never be alone in the same space with her ever again.

Which of course belies all logic, because ostensibly that’s all I ever really wanted, right?

Yeah, right.

My lawyer also told me it would be a good idea to video record the property when/if I ever did return to it, and that indeed I did do. I haven’t ever watched the full footage; there’s zero reason to, and I really should just make myself delete it. I’d sent it to my lawyer before everything zeroed out with him, and he blew it off. Told me to blow it off, too, that just about everything you had in your life as physical objects could be replaced and you VERY CLEARLY didn’t want the old life anyway SO CONGRATULATIONS you’re divorced + shit so fucking at least PRETEND like you’re happy, MOTHERFUCKER + YOU ARE WELCOME.

It wasn’t a mistake going back. I needed to see it for myself. To feel that energy. To document the destruction. And not just hers. Mostly mine. That bedroom, so small, and freshly patched where she’d slashed a huge hole in it with something she’d thrown at me that one night the fight just wouldn’t stop and it became the next morning and it still wasn’t stopped.

The mistake I made was staying a full 24 hours. 

Staying a single hour, if any shred of one at all. 

I should have driven down Sunnyside and got a room at that same place Ben Slotky had stayed when he visited us but I couldn’t have him stay with us because of the level of shame and insanity that was in play every single second of every single day when we all packed in like sardines into that Happy Valley house and pretended like we belonged there.

But of course I didn’t do that. 

I remember driving to the McDonald’s in Damascus with a load of stuff to donate/leave at the Goodwill truck that would be opening up again in the morning. It was a Sunday evening I stayed there, into the next Monday, and the lesson I learned that night was to not try and do all of it all at once. I felt the edges of my own deal that night, and that I was running so fast I couldn’t even keep up with myself. I was staying up in Salmon Creek at the time, with Ryan and Angie and Avery, and they had graciously given me an upstairs bedroom in their home to use as the ashes fell that first ferocious month. 

I’d spent 4th of July in R + A + A's house with just Mr. Chips, their beloved Scottish Terrier; the 4th was always a sore subject with W because of the games she always was playing with her never-ex husband, and she could never bring herself to tell me the truth about how she really felt about him—and me—anymore than I could do the same to tell her how I really felt about her—and someone else. When I tore down the gravel driveway and slid her Challenger onto Vogel Way that terrible morning, I didn’t know what she thought anymore and I’d stopped trying to steel myself because of that same uncertainty. Our entire life together was an abscess of the broken dreams we’d brought into the world we’d made, a world totally of fire and ice.

I know I was supposed to die in the basement of that farmhouse we never even lived a year in. Matter of fact, I DID DIE in that basement that night you saw your chance to even an old score and even get a two-fer in the process. I soft-sprained my ankle and tweaked my knee when we were at the top of the stairs and you wouldn’t stop throwing things that I guess were mine but very clearly in the end were not. Because none of it belonged to me, ultimately. 

Or to you.

I’m still plenty angry but it’s always been pointless to be mad at anyone else but my own fucking self and I know if I let all that fester and boil again, I won’t survive it again. The last time it happened, I survived and now largely wish I hadn’t. Matthew Perry wrote about this kind of delayed post-surgery self-hatred that shows up from out of fucking nowhere because I really and truly was grateful to be alive and told that to any and every one who would listen. I was in constant bargaining mode in the hospital, buying donuts and pizza and coffee and more donuts for the entire 4th floor nursing staff that took care of me and made sure I didn’t die. 

But then those wells of gratitude dried up. No particular reasons; rather, an aggregation and totalizing of all the dust I’ve stirred up all of my life, the trails and tracks that life has left in the earth and on the wind.

I don’t think I slept more than 20 minutes at a time that night in Damascus that became morning not a second too soon when the morning light burned through the big picture windows and lit up all that wood paneling. I’d spent all night there in a real-time trauma of my own making + sponging up all its bad energy. I could still hear her footsteps on the floorboards and spooked myself by taking a shower upstairs in the morning and instantly regretted it. My lawyer hadn’t negotiated breaking the lease yet, but it would happen soon enough + when it did I don’t remember feeling anything other than what I feel today, largely always will: that special, snarling sadness of a disappointment so massive you struggle to understand how it even fits on the planet, let alone all inside just you.

When I found the wedding couple with their heads so violently bent forward like you’d left them, that was the moment I knew you were completely gone from my life. Just like you’d left it. 

Not like you planned it, though. 

And certainly not like I had, either. 

The Dark: sometimes fire cannot tear through it. No matter how hot or how much fuel; some fires just will not catch, let alone burn. Sometimes even the brightest fire will not shadow some stones.

WL, I feel your ghost every time I drive Foster Road to Towle, just past where I'm living now, where you undoubtedly cut through on that long drive back to the Snake River Plain where we all came from but not really. When I took that townhouse in Happy Valley, I didn’t have much choice but to live with that same ghost until it nearly took me down to the riverbed to drown. “You should run,” you always told me. And, of course, when I did, you could’t believe that I'd ever really heard you in the first place.

## BUT SHE’S TOUCHING HIS CHEST NOW HE TAKES OFF HER DRESS NOW LET ME GO.
-THE KILLERS, 2004.
------

It’s funny how I see your name on the screen up there above that stupid line from that stupid Killers song + still feel largely nothing. I typed it myself. You know? Put it there *on purpose*? I don’t remember why and it definitely doesn’t matter. But it’s still funny.

And so were you. The entire way, top to bottom. You were funny about all of it and even sometimes during it. Like how we stopped at the top of Johnson Hill and looked out across Big Camas Prairie and you pretended to want to be there with me and I pretended like I wanted to be there at all. We might have side-hugged at the overlook, our hands still dry from showering at your place that same morning, nowhere close by on purpose. 

We corkscrewed your SUV down the grade and onto the prairie and continued following the highway up from Wendell and through Gooding, across those thin railroad tracks and then turning left onto Highway 20, which didn’t yet have all those Medal of Honor markers on it yet. At least not through Bannock country yet, is my guess. We’d just stopped at the overlook but you wanted to stop at the only grocery store in whatever you’d call Fairfield, ID, and then some more in front of an old railroad caboose next to a gazebo that looked a lot like the one MFUP would build for us to head fake each other with only a year later but I wasn’t even remotely thinking about proposing to you, let alone talking myself into it and talking myself into it and talking myself into it like I would be mere months later, when summer was over again and our lives had to fold back up into whatever crazy origami it required. 

It was October and winter was coming and you were letting go of what you could let go of and that’s why we were in a jewelry store at all that day in October and that’s why you were the one who proposed to me in one of the weirdest and most manipulative ways possible: you’d buy your own fucking ring, rather than wait for something you never wanted in the first place.

So you did.

I don’t think I’ve ever caught back up to who I was before that moment. Nor do I want to. If anything, I’m putting more and more distance between whomever that was and whatever I’ve become now. The lines aren’t straight; the connections are frayed at best; almost everything that is and ever was is far more complication than one cause, one effect; the truth is more like a ripple than a wave, anyway.

You kept buying the ring a secret from everyone except me, and then literally made me hold it. You flew in to PDX with it still in its box and sat across from me at a restaurant in Cascade Station, batting your eyes and stroking my fingers and waiting for me to put the box in my pocket and pretend like it all wasn’t really exactly the way it was but we’d never ever say.

Instead, we agreed we’d keep it secret. 

We’d hang on to that tiny little thread that’d started it all, that stupid friend request I’d sent, that terrible record of my loneliness and hopelessness that had metastasized into the huge and gaudy thing that never quite looked real and always looked like cubic zirconium if I’m being completely honest, that terrible objective correlative wrapped around your finger and encrusted with flecks and shards of diamond, composited and shaped all together as if to give the illusion of something whole and complete.

What I’m trying to say, my dear, is this: you always had terrible taste. And not just in jewelry. In everything. 

Especially in men. Especially Me.

## TAKE YOUR TIME + TELL ME I'M WRONG.
-MAMMOTH, 2025.
------------

When do you know you’ve written it all, let alone the last word?

It’s Memorial Day 2026 as I’m typing this, WL. There’s not a single mistake that makes any of this any different. It’s been 7 years since that terrible morning we birthed something even more terrible and let it windigo everything we’d ever pretended mattered between us. I see you every day of the commutes I don’t need to even make in the first place, driving that same stretch of Foster Road along the county line we lived on for those last 9 months and god if those weren’t the worst of all + baby did we have some super super bad times.

Mostly, even.

The emotional calculus I’ll never be able to do on paper, let alone in my head. My heart can’t compute anything and never could; stillborn, that muscle even still, but the rest of me had to trudge on + even still.

And whatever. I can’t regret what we did and not just that last day but all of them before it. Every single one.

Like that very first time you strutted through security and as soon as our eyes locked I knew I wanted every crevice of you because night had already fallen into morning + by the time we got to The Place it was something like almost 2 am.

Or like that very last time your strut was way slower and you were dragging two Achilles heels across that new parking lot out by DeJardin and you knew I was watching you because you gave me the double bird once you’d hopped in your Challenger + read what I’d left in there for you + I still will never regret filming the whole fucking thing.

One good turn deserves another was something we decided gave us the right to be shitty. 

Another thing we decided along the way was to let God sort it all out.

Two wrongs don’t make a right; however, they make for some more than interesting half-truths.

I know, I know, I know. You always hated this particular part of me. The rhetorical and even anti-social part of me that is still always going to be just tossing a football up in the air for myself to catch, just like on the big lawns of my parents’ house on Jefferson St and my grandparents’ house on Clinton Dr and even in the gopher-mounded summer grasses on River Dr with that enormous blue sky and sun so bright it is quite really not even a little yellow but completely white like bleach is white, all overhead, blinding me as I would toss that fat football PLARD still had from the building and drinking days that quickly just became the drinking days; when the place in Featherville was built, it was an every weekend excuse to cut up to the cabin and cut loose like they’ve always done in Alturas County since before there ever even was an Alturas County in the first place.

But something else you know: it’s rare I don’t get back underneath the football by time it comes back down now, aint it?

Because this is about endings, ultimately. Everything gets to be about endings as some point, right? Everything comes to an end, all that shit. Right? 

Ghosts, though? Where and what are they? What brings them? What sends them away? Are they even real? Why wouldn’t they be real? 

What a ghost is, is a question that can never be fully answered.

What a ghost is, is a memory that can never be fully erased. No matter how many times you’ve tried telling yourself its story, there’s at least that one shard, that one fragment of it, that just won’t dissolve. 

What a ghost is, is when your breath pauses because something in your head has dislodged and traveled directly to your heart.

What a ghost is, is when you go to clutch your heart but your hand ends up flat against your forehead instead.

What a ghost is, is faith that the past as you’ve been told it is close enough to something called The Truth that the people you love will stop leaving in all of the ghastliest ways people can leave each other.

What a ghost is, is also the loss of that same faith.

What a ghost is, is comma splices in the dark.

What a ghost is, is audiences of ones and zeroes.

What a ghost is, is an orphan.

What a ghost is, makes all of us widowers.

What a ghost is, is 31 Dec 1934.

What a ghost is, is 17 Mar 1945.

What a ghost is, is 1945, in all of its bliss and despair.

What a ghost is, is 1961 which is 1942 which is 1951 which is one last time forever.

What a ghost is, is July 11, 2011.

What a ghost isn’t, is July 11, 2025.

What a ghost isn’t, is filling my lungs back up with the air all around me and continuing to breathe.

What a ghost isn’t, is a heart that won’t stop beating until it has literally bound itself up and stopped working, just trying to keep the next beat something I can actually feel.

What a ghost isn’t, is any distinction between what’s real and what’s not; you’d have to be completely kidding to try and argue otherwise.

What a ghost isn’t, is plowing through recent photographs of River Dr and thinking I’d seen myself trapped there like Clora must have felt at the very very very last second still, and because she still felt that way, well, you know what happened next.

Right?

All of the other endings, WL, they all end the same way: we continue fighting. For eternity. Sealed in it, even. There is enough rage between us to fuel it for at least a million more lifetimes.

Want to? HUGE parts of me do. Always have. Always will.

You are not a ghost. None of these people are. People can’t be ghosts, I’ve decided. And that includes you.

When you sang for me, I know it wasn’t for me. I always knew it wasn’t and that’s why I just let go on the railing that night and sank all the way to the bottom of the world. So far down, all the light blinked off and sound drowned out, below where even the beginning of whatever cold is and comes from, comes from. I’d hoped the Sea of Cortez would just swallow us that day but it didn’t. You’d already stopped singing for me, I guess is what I’m saying. I was there to drown. 

So I did.

I disappeared in you. And you are not a ghost. I don’t know what you are. I never did. It doesn’t matter. None of it ever did.

Because the truth as close as I can get to telling it goes like this: none of us should have ever been here in the first place.

I got my own breath to prove it.

I had infinities to choose between that night bleeding into dawn, July 10, 2022. I adopted myself, chose myself. Raised myself. Educated myself. Bettered myself. Humbled myself. Dared myself. Scared myself. Destroyed myself.

Again.

+ 

Again.

What a ghost isn’t, is the number 9, no matter how different Emma might have been when she was back at school the day after they buried Clora after they cleared a snow drift wide enough in Shoshone in time enough to get all those Chess kids back to Priest Road that night in time to get up for school the next day, 9-year-old Emma staring through the walls and burning holes through them and dreaming of fire when she can’t sleep and daydreaming of it when she can.

What a ghost isn’t, is also the number 9, but because time is indeed a train and makes the future the past, it’s 1932 and it’s June 4 and Emma is 7 and Clora is still young and beautiful and posing for a photograph, even, with her hair done and wearing a blue dress and wearing a simple cameo on an even simpler chain, and because it was Clora’s it never ever ever left Idaho not even one time and never would and Emma made that promise when they pushed her back into the car and she had to drive all the way back to Priest Road knowing how cold it would always be right there where Clora couldn’t ever wake up to warm herself. 

Nope, it’s none of that. It’s 1932 and it’s Earl’s baptism day and he’s at the center of the universe, Langston Hughes style, on the mourner’s bench with no mourners. Not yet anyway. Their entire lives ahead of them as the cliches go, but a cliche becomes a cliche for completely understandable if not predictable reasons.

It’s 1932 and the world isn’t perfect and will never be. It will be worse and always will be. 

But not entirely.

Because it’s not 1932 anywhere else BUT 1932. 

And it’s not even July 11, 2025 anymore.

Soon, it’ll be September again.

And it’ll be somebody’s birthday again.

But every day is somebody’s birthday, just like every day is somebody’s death day.

We should be as lucky to count each one what it really really really fucking is no matter what and if this isn’t true then nothing ever is/was/will be.

Because what IS every day really really really? No matter what? Always? Forever? All that?

A goddamn miracle is what. 

An absolute and irrefutable miracle. 

And you are welcome.

-AUG 12 2025, GRESHAM OREGON AT THE LIP OF AN EVEN MORE ANCIENT VOLCANO AND BUT MOSTLY IN DEFERENCE TO THE GREAT AND MIGHTY FIRST NATION CITIZENS OF THIS OTHERWISE COUNTRY INFESTED BY SQUATTERS AND OWLFACED BITCHES LIKE YOURS TRULY; I DEDICATE THIS DRAFT TO CLORA ALICE DAVIS MAY SHE FOREVER REST IN PEACE AND OUR LOVE.

T.
